After fifty years on the water, I still get fired up about how much federal land we have out West — BLM, Forest Service, millions of acres that belong to all of us.

But here's the part a lot of newcomers miss, and it's important:

Public land ≠ open minerals everywhere.

You can hike, camp, and enjoy a whole lot of ground as the public. Extracting gold is a different question. Much of the creek bottom and bench ground you'd want to work is already tied up in active mining claims — sometimes marked with posts and corner tags, sometimes not obvious until you've done your homework.

Before you shovel, research it: check BLM claim records, state databases, maps in your guide, and on-the-ground markers. If it's claimed and it isn't yours (or a GPAA claim you're cleared to use), stay off. Respect the claim. Find unclaimed open ground or ground where you have permission.

On ground that is truly open to casual prospecting, the day-to-day rules are usually straightforward:

  • Hand pan + shovel? Often fine on open federal ground — still check local notices.
  • Metal detecting? Allowed in many areas; not everywhere — read the district rules.
  • Small sluice on the bank? Common on BLM/FS where minerals aren't locked up — verify first.
  • Camp while you prospect? 14-day stay limits, then move down the road.

Always steer clear of National Parks, monuments, tribal lands, wilderness restrictions, and water where dredging is prohibited.

Your guide includes a 2025 state-by-state snapshot (Alaska to Georgia) because rules shift like creek levels.
